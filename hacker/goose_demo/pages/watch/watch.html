      Best Watches Under $5,000

      15 Stylish Yet Affordable Watches Guaranteed To Turn Heads

      Amos Kwon
      April 24, 2017
      Share
      Tweet
      0
      Shares

      The sheer array of good watches out there is enough to make us drool, give us a headache or both. Spend as little as $200, and you can get yourself a sensible Japanese automatic dive watch that will last you years. Drop $10,000, and you can buy an heirloom-quality timepiece that your kids will fight over. Halve that at $5K, and you’ve reached the point at which top-quality watches begin. But where do you even begin to select something that’s more than most people’s monthly salary and should stand apart from the glut of $1,000 timepieces out there?

      Here’s some sagely wisdom to guide you. First, don’t buy anything with a battery. Aim for a watch that has a Swiss mechanical movement. Aside from materials, this is what you pay for. And you can choose between an old-school hand-wound mechanical movement or a self-winding mechanical automatic. And don’t assume that it’s all about the brand, either. You can sometimes get more watch for your money if you go with a smaller, lesser known brand that still has a stellar reputation in watch circles for quality, accuracy and style. Here are fifteen selections that should provide years of great timekeeping and a fair amount of jealousy. Some of our selections go for less than $2,000, while others graze that magical $5,000 mark.

      Ball Engineer Master II Diver

      The Ball brand is born out of the need for ultra-precise railroad timekeeping, and we consider it the thinking man’s watch brand since their models are unique, incredibly well made and achingly precise. The Master II Diver gets a brilliantly made inner bezel ring and super-bright self-illuminating tritium gas tubes. The ETA 2836 movement is about as accurate as they come, giving the Engineer moniker serious teeth.
      $2,399.00 at Nordstrom.com

      Torgoen T32

      If you’ve never heard of this Swiss watch brand, you’re not alone. They’re a niche pilot watch manufacturer whose founder is a pilot himself, so you see the direct inspiration. The T32 was their first automatic, and it’s more of a pilot’s gauges-style than a true aviation watch. The handsome flat black dial and black case are accentuated nicely by orange highlights on the face and second hand, and a stitched leather strap. The Swiss automatic Valanvron 24 movement is subbed out for the more restricted ETA movements, and it’s nicely viewable through the rear glass caseback. And like a good pilot’s watch, the lume is pretty killer.
      $834.00 at Amazon.com

      Cartier Tank Francaise Automatic

      Birthed as a new member of the Cartier Tank family in 1995, the Francaise is a watch that will never look dated. With a bracelet that’s meant to mimic tank caterpillars, it’s true to the namesake. The blued hands and cabochon crown punctuate the iconic Roman numeral dial, and the Swiss automatic movement masters the timekeeping function. It’s elegance embodied, without any hint of gauche.
      $3,849.00 at Amazon.com

      Nomos Glashutte Ahoi Atlantik Automatic

      In what amounts to Nomos’ sportiest watch, the deep blue dial and cloth band still exude sophistication, albeit at a more casual level than other Nomos models. The handsome seconds subdial gets a simple but eye-catching red hand, and the 40mm case and 200-meter water resistance means you can wear it in the office and the pool with equal aplomb.
      $3,495.00 at Amazon.com

      Breitling Aeromarine Superocean Heritage

      Breitling’s version of the Rolex Submariner is arguably handsomer and more sophisticated thanks to its gorgeous mesh bracelet, Superocean lettering and numberless bezel. Of course, like all Breitlings, it’s more than just skin deep goodness. The depth rating is a serious 200 meters, way deeper than your aquatic adventures, the Swiss automatic movement is spot on, and it’s a genuine certified chronometer.
      $3,275.00 at Amazon.com

      Tutima Grand Classic F2 Chronograph

      Glashutte, Germany’s Tutima is a brand that doesn’t get nearly enough visibility, and that’s too bad because their watches are worth far more than their asking price. Such is the case with the F2, a Flieger-style chronograph with a stunning coin edge fixed bezel and gorgeous, intricate Squelette hands.
      $892.96 at Amazon.com

      Oris Atelier Automatic Calibre 111

      One of Oris’s more expensive timepieces is a hand-wound beauty and makes use of their in-house movement that has a whopping 240-hour power reserve that shows up on the bi-color subdial. It’s versatile dress watch thanks to its larger 43mm case that wears flat. It also has Superluminova that makes it perfect for low-light conditions, a very non-dress watch characteristic, indeed.
      $4,300.00 at Amazon.com

      Bell & Ross Vintage BR 126 Sport Heritage GMT & Flyback

      When a new timepiece executes vintage perfectly, it looks exactly like this. The golden-brown color of the hands, markers and numerals against the black dial are stunning, capped off by a beveled, flat sapphire crystal. The Swiss GMT chrono movement ensures high accuracy and seamless travel, while the brown leather strap caps off the classic look.
      $4,495.00 at Amazon.com

      Tissot PRS 516

      This blackened version of the venerable Swiss made PRS 516 is one of our favorites thanks to the black carbon fiber dial and blue contrast hands and racing strap stitching. Its PVD case has a subtle matte finish, and the automatic chronograph movement with 54-hour power reserve is worthy of race car lap timing. It’s one of those timepieces that communicates how serious you are about sporting.
      $384.79 at Amazon.com

      Alpina Adventure Sailing Chronograph

      Though this regatta watch is meant for those who competitively sail, it’s not reserved for them. Owners of this special watch can boast a color timer that counts down in ten minute increments using bright colors in cutouts just below and between the 10 and 2 o’clock markers. But it also operates as a bonafide diving watch with its unidirectional bezel and 300 meter depth rating.
      $892.86 at Amazon.com

      Junghans Meister Kalendar

      The Kalendar is a stunning achievement in the way it melds old and new school. The triple calendar dial and moonphase dial that’s undergirded by the in-house J800.3 movement. Its gorgeous gold-plated PVD case is 40.4mm in diameter and capped off by a curved plexiglass crystal (similar to traditional acrylic versions) that’s been beefed up with added scratch resistance. All we know is the damned thing is a jawdropper.
      $2,320.00 at Amazon.com

      Longines Heritage Military

      Longines is rolling out vintage homage pieces, and this one’s a winner. Inspired by a WWII British military piece, the Heritage Military barely changes its look from the handsome original except it gets upsized from 36 to 40mm and has a smaller crown. The stainless steel case houses a reliable Swiss ETA movement and the hands and markers get bright Superluminova C3 markers for those clandestine missions. It’s one of the best casual field watches we’ve seen in a while.
      $1,530.00 at Amazon.com

      Louis Erard Excellence Automatic

      One of the most appealing dress watches we’ve seen in a long time happens to come from an independent Swiss brand that’s been around since 1931. The Excellence Automatic’s white dial is peppered by rose-gold hands, markers and numerals, giving lush contrast and sophistication to the 42mm case. The ETA 7751 Swiss automatic movement incorporates two subdials and a date wheel. It’s a watch you’ll be proud to wear despite the fact that few will know the brand, but many will ask.
      $3,495.00 at Amazon.com

      Tudor North Flag Automatic

      It’s a worthy watch to get Tudor’s first in-house movement, a monumental step after the brand’s reintroduction into the U.S. watch market in 2013. The North Flag has a unique face with bold hands and markers, along with a simple yellow power reserve gauge. The integrated bracelet is beautifully designed, and the 40mm diameter is the perfect, classic size that toes the line so well between dress and sport.
      $3,301.00 at Amazon.com

      Bremont Supermarine S500 BK

      Don’t mistake this for just a semi-pricey dive watch, because the British S500 is so much more. Built like Fort Knox, this watch is not only tough but also with the kind of specs worthy of a feat of engineering. The 43mm hardened steel case houses a Faraday cage that’s anti-shock and anti-magnetic. It has a skull-crushing 500 meter depth rating and the sapphire glass crystal has an unreal 9 layers of anti-reflection coating on each side. The in-house BE-36AE automatic movement is chronometer certified, making it the real deal. The fact that it’s Bremont’s first dive watch is pretty hard to believe in light of all this goodness.
      $3,860.00 at Amazon.com
